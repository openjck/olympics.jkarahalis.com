EDU 5721: Contract Negotiations in Education

  Spring 2010                                                                                                              Dr. Jonathan T. Hughes

The course co-taught by Dr. Jonathan T. Hughes and Dr. Joan M.  Hughes introduced the important aspect of collective bargaining as it relates to school based contracts, school boards and teacher unions.  The differences between distributive bargaining where there must be a winner and a loser and integrative bargaining in which a win, win resolution is possible. 

In the case of distributive bargaining one must be cognizant of the Best Alternative To a Negotiated Contract (B.A.T.N.A.). In any successful negotiating it is also imperative to get good representation on the team making it critical to pick the right team. In addition members of the negotiating team must also be learned in federal and state law as well as contract language and past practices.  Subjects of bargaining include mandatory subjects, prohibitive subjects and with permission from the teams, non-mandatory subjects.

The 7 elements of effective negotiations include the following:

 

  1. Communication
  2. Relationships
  3. Interests
  4. Options
  5. Legitimacy
  6. Commitment
  7. Alternatives

 

As a resource the book “Getting To Yes” (Fisher & Patton (1981) allowed members of my Cohort to ascertain that it is essential to separate the people from the problem. The key areas as described by Dr. Hughes included:   Perceptions, emotions, communication and prevention.

 The second part of our course involved assigning my cohort members and me one of two negotiating groups (teachers union and school board) that were responsible in formulating a new contract for teachers in our district. Dr. Hughes assigned our roles based on the opposite of our positions we presently hold.  For example: not holding an administrative position as a School Psychologist I was placed on the school board negotiating team.  In this exercise I was not only given first hand experience of how contract negotiations are conducted but become enlightened to the perspective of a group whose point of view I never considered.  At the conclusion of the weekend exercise which eventually appeared to end in agreement on many issues I now gained a newfound respect for the contract negotiation process that I hope to become firmly entrenched someday as a future school administrator.
