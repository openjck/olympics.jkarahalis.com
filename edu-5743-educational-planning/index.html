EDU 5743: Educational Planning

Summer 2007
Dr. Korynne-Taylor Dunlop

On July 6, 2007 Cohort 8 initiated a journey into the doctoral program. Dr. Dunlop’s warm and welcoming manner helped to ease some of the nervous apprehension we all experienced to some degree, and introduced the goals and challenges before us. The focus of the course was on planned change in education, both conceptually and practically in school settings. We were charged with applying different frameworks and theories to examine the change process, to analyze organizational problems, ant to develop constructive strategies for change. We underscored the importance of applying theories to examine the process of organizational change in education and emphasized that good theory is the means by which practitioners gain clear perspectives and direction.

Cohort members examined theoretical frameworks via the text, Sustainable Leadership (Hargreaves & Fink, 2006). The seven essential principles of sustainability outlined by Hargreaves and Fink are:

  1. Depth: Sustainable leadership matters. It is important to preserve, protect and promote in education what itself sustainable as an enrichment is of life.
  2. Length: Sustainable leadership lasts. It preserves and advances the most valuable aspects of life over time.
  3. Breadth: Sustainable leadership spreads. It sustains as well as depends on the leadership of others.
  4. Justice: Sustainable leadership does no harm to and actively improves the surrounding environment. It is not self-centered; it is socially just.
  5. Diversity: Sustainable leadership promotes cohesive diversity. It fosters and learns from diversity in teaching and learning and moves things forward by creating cohesion and networking among its richly varied components.
  6. Resourcefulness: Sustainable leadership develops and does not deplete material and human resources. It renews people’s energy and does not drain its leaders dry through innovation overload or unrealistic time lines for change.
  7. Conservation: Sustainable leadership honors and learns from the best of the past to create an even better future. It is steadfast about preserving and renewing its longstanding purposes.

The conclusions drawn from the studies of Hargreaves and Fink were that change in education is easy to propose, hard to implement and extraordinarily difficult to sustain.  Thus, it is in the best interest of future leaders to see our goals as change agents and to include a framework of sustainability which will make our efforts worthwhile and long-lasting.

In addition to the text, students studied other theorists regarding “change” in education. These included but were not limited to: Schein, Popekewitz, Wehlage, and Smith. Using Frank Smith’s IOGA design Cohort members presented a matrix of major theorists with organization of the four elements of school design.

Key ideas considered throughout the course included:

  • What does it mean to know?
  • What does it mean to work?
  • What is the structure of authority?

Overall themes throughout the course incorporated:

  • Change is a journey not a blueprint.
  • Don’t confuse motion with progress.
  • Paradox: change is the one constant in life.

As our Cohort completed its first course we were reminded by Dr. Dunlop to value each member of working in a constructivist community together.  Dr. Dunlop provided students with what was to follow as we completed our first course and how to succeed as students in this Doctoral program.

Textbook

  • Hargreaves, A.  & Fink, D. (2006) Sustainable Leadership.  San Francisco, CA: Jossey-Bass
